Audit of text-to-image engines · DALL-E × Imagen

Who does the machine imagine?

Hundreds of AI-generated portraits were produced from neutral occupational prompts — athlete, paramedic, neurosurgeon, tech CEO — then coded for apparent race and gender. The result is a synthetic population that looks almost nothing like the real one.

Study 02 · Semantic drift · 8 LLMs × 6 professions · 29 tests

Same job. Add one adjective. Watch the person change.

Eight language models were asked to depict the same professions three ways — neutral, "highly skilled", and "lowly skilled" — and the gender and race of each generated character was coded. All labels were normalized first ("Black" = "African American" = "African"), because the raw shift flags counted terminology swaps as demographic changes.

S.01Race × prestige · all tests

Black share tracks the prestige dial — p ≈ 0.001

Black characters make up 62.1% of "highly skilled" depictions, 44.8% of neutral ones, and just 24.1% of "lowly skilled" ones. Comparing highly→lowly directly: 11 tests dropped the Black character, 0 adopted one — exact sign test p = 0.00098. The single statistically strongest result in either study.

S.02Where "lowly skilled" goes

Low skill is offloaded onto Asian and Latina characters — every single time

Of the 8 tests where the character stopped being Black under "lowly skilled" framing, 100% landed on Asian (5) or Latina/o (3). Zero went to White, zero to any other category. At lowly-skilled, Asian becomes the single largest category (31.0%) — up from 13.8% under "highly skilled."

S.03Gender × prestige

Women aren't replaced when skill drops. They're erased. (p = 0.016)

Female share falls from 82.8% (highly skilled) and 75.9% (neutral) to 51.7% at lowly skilled — 7 tests left "female," 0 entered it, sign test p = 0.016. But the destination isn't "male": it's "unspecified," which nearly triples to 37.9%. Models name a gender for excellence and go vague for incompetence.

S.05Models & data quality

Half the reported "gender drift" was terminology noise — and the models differ wildly

After normalizing labels, race shift drops from 79.3% (raw) to 72.4%, but gender shift collapses from 65.5% to 37.9% — "woman"→"female" was being counted as drift. Among models: gpt-4.1 is most stable (42.9% race / 14.3% gender), claude-4-sonnet shifts race in 5/5 tests, and gpt-5.2 deflected the "lowly skilled" prompt in 5/5 tests — the only safety-correct behavior, at the cost of corrupting its cells.

Method

Study 01: neutral occupational prompts submitted to two text-to-image engines (DALL-E, Imagen); outputs coded for apparent race and gender (athlete n=227, paramedic n=271). Study 02: eight LLMs asked to depict six professions under neutral, "highly skilled," and "lowly skilled" framing (29 tests); coded labels normalized before analysis to merge synonyms. Significance: exact binomial tests where counts are known, paired exact sign tests for Study 02 transitions, conservative one-proportion bounds elsewhere — see Appendix B.

Caveats

"Apparent" race and gender are coder judgments, not identities. U.S. baselines are approximate; "all other" rows are arithmetic remainders. Study 02: n=29 with one sample per cell, 56% coverage of the model×profession grid, six "lowly" cells affected by model refusals, and one multi-option baseline arbitrarily coded. Only findings marked SIGNIFICANT in Appendix B clear α = 0.05; the rest are directional. Figures describe these systems, these prompts, these runs — not all AI.
